1. Order of Operations

Following this strict mathematical sequence ensures that any given numeric expression evaluates to a single, universally consistent value.

Mathematical expressions are evaluated sequentially using the PEMDAS (or GEMS) hierarchy:

  • Parentheses / Grouping Symbols (evaluate nested bounds first)
  • Exponents
  • Multiplication & Division (from left to right)
  • Addition & Subtraction (from left to right)

2. Properties of Real Numbers

Commutative Property

This property asserts that the order in which you add or multiply numbers does not alter the final sum or product.

\[ a + b = b + a \] \[ a \cdot b = b \cdot a \]

Associative Property

This property states that changing the grouping of values during addition or multiplication does not change the result.

\[ (a + b) + c = a + (b + c) \] \[ (a \cdot b) \cdot c = a \cdot (b \cdot c) \]

Distributive Property

This property allows you to multiply a factor outside a set of parentheses by every individual term contained inside.

\[ a(b + c) = ab + ac \]

Identity & Inverse

Identity properties keep a number's value intact, while inverse properties combine a number with its opposite or reciprocal to return the identity element.

\[ a + 0 = a \quad \text{and} \quad a \cdot 1 = a \] \[ a + (-a) = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad a \cdot \frac{1}{a} = 1 \]

3. Rules of Signed Numbers

Addition Rules

Adding signed values works like movement on a coordinate line, where negative inputs pull leftward and positive inputs pull rightward.

  • Same Signs: Sum absolute values and keep the shared sign.
  • Different Signs: Find the difference of absolute values. Apply the sign of the larger absolute value.

Multiplication & Division

Multiplying or dividing terms with matching signs always yields a positive result, whereas mismatched signs result in a negative value.

Operation Resulting Sign Example
$\text{Pos} \times \text{Pos}$ $\text{Positive}$ $3 \times 4 = 12$
$\text{Neg} \times \text{Neg}$ $\text{Positive}$ $-3 \times -4 = 12$
$\text{Pos} \times \text{Neg}$ $\text{Negative}$ $3 \times -4 = -12$

4. Operations with Fractions

Addition & Subtraction

Finding a common denominator scales the fractions so that you are combining pieces of equal size.

\[ \frac{a}{c} \pm \frac{b}{c} = \frac{a \pm b}{c} \]

Multiplication

Multiplying fractions simply requires scaling the top parts together and the bottom parts together directly, without needing a common denominator.

\[ \frac{a}{b} \cdot \frac{c}{d} = \frac{a \cdot c}{b \cdot d} \]

Division

Dividing by a fraction is equivalent to scaling by its reciprocal, often memorized as "keeping" the first, "changing" the sign, and "flipping" the second.

\[ \frac{a}{b} \div \frac{c}{d} = \frac{a}{b} \cdot \frac{d}{c} = \frac{ad}{bc} \]

5. Properties of Equality

These fundamental axioms ensure that any operation applied equally to both sides of an equation preserves its mathematical balance.

If $a = b$, then the following hold true for any real number $c$:

  • Addition: $a + c = b + c$
  • Subtraction: $a - c = b - c$
  • Multiplication: $a \cdot c = b \cdot c$
  • Division: $\frac{a}{c} = \frac{b}{c} \quad (c \neq 0)$
  • Reflexive: $a = a$
  • Symmetric: If $a = b$, then $b = a$
  • Transitive: If $a = b$ and $b = c$, then $a = c$

6. Rules for Inequalities

Reversing the inequality sign is essential because multiplying or dividing by a negative number mirrors the relative positions of values across zero on a number line, swapping their order.

Standard properties of equality apply to inequalities ($<$, $>$, $\le$, $\ge$), with one exception:

Sign Flip Rule: When multiplying or dividing both sides of an inequality by a negative number, the direction of the inequality sign must be reversed.

Example: Given $-3x < 9$, dividing both sides by $-3$ yields $x > -3$.

7. Absolute Value Rules

Since absolute value represents physical distance from zero on a coordinate line, its output must always be positive or zero.

Absolute value evaluates a real number's non-negative distance from zero:

  • $|a| \ge 0$
  • If $|x| = a$ (where $a \ge 0$), then $x = a$ or $x = -a$
  • $|a \cdot b| = |a| \cdot |b|$
  • $\left| \frac{a}{b} \right| = \frac{|a|}{|b|} \quad (b \neq 0)$

8. Rules of Radicals (Roots)

These rules hold true for positive real bases:

Product Rule

The product rule enables you to split a radical of a product into individual factors, or combine separate radicals of the same index into one.

\[ \sqrt[n]{ab} = \sqrt[n]{a} \cdot \sqrt[n]{b} \]

Quotient Rule

The quotient rule allows you to distribute a radical sign to both the numerator and the denominator of a fraction individually.

\[ \sqrt[n]{\frac{a}{b}} = \frac{\sqrt[n]{a}}{\sqrt[n]{b}} \]

9. Graphing & Linear Equations

Slope ($m$) and Forms

These mathematical models describe straight lines on a coordinate plane by linking variables $x$ and $y$ using a constant rate of change and reference points.

\[ m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} \] \[ y = mx + b \quad \text{(Slope-Intercept Form)} \] \[ y - y_1 = m(x - x_1) \quad \text{(Point-Slope Form)} \] \[ Ax + By = C \quad \text{(Standard Form)} \]

Parallel: Slopes are identical ($m_1 = m_2$)
Perpendicular: Slopes are negative reciprocals ($m_1 \cdot m_2 = -1$)

10. Factoring & Polynomials

Trinomials & Squares

These special product equations provide shortcuts to expand squared expressions or quickly factor a difference of perfect squares back into simpler binomials.

\[ a^2 - b^2 = (a - b)(a + b) \] \[ a^2 + 2ab + b^2 = (a + b)^2 \]

Quadratic Formula

The quadratic formula provides the exact mathematical coordinate roots where any second-degree polynomial crosses the x-axis.

For any quadratic equation in the form $ax^2 + bx + c = 0$:

\[ x = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} \]

11. Rules of Exponents

These mathematical laws define how factors scale when multiplied, divided, raised to nested powers, or evaluated with rational and negative values.

These properties apply to bases where $a \neq 0$ and $b \neq 0$:

Rule Name Algebraic Formula Example
Product Rule $a^m \cdot a^n = a^{m+n}$ $3^2 \cdot 3^4 = 3^6 = 729$
Quotient Rule $\frac{a^m}{a^n} = a^{m-n}$ $\frac{5^7}{5^4} = 5^3 = 125$
Power of a Power $(a^m)^n = a^{m \cdot n}$ $(2^3)^4 = 2^{12} = 4{,}096$
Power of a Product $(ab)^n = a^n b^n$ $(2x)^3 = 8x^3$
Power of a Quotient $\left(\frac{a}{b}\right)^n = \frac{a^n}{b^n}$ $\left(\frac{3}{4}\right)^2 = \frac{9}{16}$
Zero Exponent $a^0 = 1$ $15^0 = 1$
Negative Exponent $a^{-n} = \frac{1}{a^n}$ $4^{-2} = \frac{1}{16}$
Fractional Exponent $a^{\frac{m}{n}} = \sqrt[n]{a^m}$ $8^{\frac{2}{3}} = (\sqrt[3]{8})^2 = 4$
